Sarah considered herself progressive. She cared about climate change, supported public healthcare, and believed in social justice. But when a friend asked her opinion on a specific education policy proposal, she froze. She hadn't really thought about it. Yet she felt pressure to defend it anyway—because that's what progressives were supposed to support, right? She found herself arguing for a position she didn't fully understand, simply because it came from "her side."

This happens more than we'd like to admit. We adopt labels—progressive, conservative, libertarian, feminist, environmentalist—and then find ourselves defending entire belief systems we never consciously chose. The label becomes the identity, and the identity demands loyalty.

This isn't a new problem. It's not even a social media problem. Psychologists have been watching it happen under controlled conditions for decades.

## How Easily We Split Into Tribes

In the 1950s, psychologist Muzafer Sherif ran an experiment at Robbers Cave State Park in Oklahoma. He took two groups of 11-year-old boys to summer camp and randomly divided them into two groups: the Eagles and the Rattlers. These kids didn't know each other beforehand. The division was completely arbitrary.

Within days, the groups developed fierce loyalty to their own team and hostility toward the other. They raided each other's cabins, burned flags, and hurled insults. Sherif had to end some of the planned competitions early because the conflict got too intense. All it took was giving them different team names.

Two decades later, Henri Tajfel took this even further. In his minimal group experiments, he divided people into groups based on trivial criteria—whether they preferred paintings by Klee or Kandinsky, or even just a coin flip. Then he had them distribute rewards between group members.

The results were striking. People consistently favored their own group, even when the group membership meant nothing. Even when giving more to the other group would have meant everyone got more overall, people chose to maximize the difference in favor of their group. They'd rather their group get less, as long as the other group got even less than that.

These experiments show something uncomfortable: we're wired to favor the ingroup over the outgroup, even when the distinction is meaningless and even when it works against our own interests.

## The Manipulation Game

Once you've accepted a group label, you become vulnerable. Political operatives, media personalities, and activists know this. They use phrases like "people like us believe..." or "if you're really one of us, you'd support..." It's a loyalty test disguised as information.

This creates cognitive dissonance. You might have doubts about a policy, but questioning it feels like betraying your tribe. So you rationalize. You find reasons to support what you're supposed to support. You defend positions you arrived at through peer pressure, not through independent thought.

The label becomes a straightjacket. Any nuance gets treated as disloyalty.

## The Problem With Labeling Others

It works both ways. When we label someone else as "a liberal" or "a conservative," we project an entire belief system onto them—one they might not actually hold. We're not arguing with the person in front of us anymore. We're arguing with a caricature.

I've watched conversations derail because someone says, "Well, you're a conservative, so you must think..." And the other person responds, "I never said that." It doesn't matter. The label has been applied, and now they're stuck defending positions they don't hold.

This prevents real dialogue. Instead of discussing the specific idea in front of us, we're shadow-boxing with stereotypes.

## When Groups Make Us Dumber

Important issues don't fit neatly into tribal categories. Climate policy involves economics, technology, international cooperation, and ethics. Healthcare reform requires understanding insurance markets, medical outcomes, and budget constraints. Immigration policy is tangled up with labor markets, human rights, security concerns, and cultural change.

None of these issues have a "progressive answer" or a "conservative answer" that applies to every question. They require actual thinking—looking at evidence, considering trade-offs, and accepting that smart people can disagree.

But tribal thinking short-circuits all of that. Instead of asking "what does the evidence suggest?" we ask "what does my team believe?" We judge policies by who proposes them, not by what they'd actually do.

This is how you get situations where both sides end up worse off. Government shutdowns. Gridlock on problems everyone agrees need solving. Refusing to support good ideas because they came from the wrong team.

When group victory matters more than good outcomes, everyone loses.

## The Race to the Bottom

Group psychology pushes us toward extremes. If you're in a group of like-minded people, discussions don't moderate your views—they amplify them. Researchers call this group polarization. You hear your beliefs echoed back, and it emboldens you to take more extreme positions.

Meanwhile, anyone who expresses doubt gets treated with suspicion. Are they really one of us? Dissent becomes dangerous. Innovation requires disagreement, but disagreement threatens group cohesion.

The result is a race to the bottom. Both sides become more rigid, more certain, more hostile. Compromise looks like weakness. The other side becomes not just wrong, but evil.

## Breaking Free

So what do we do instead?

**Define yourself by values, not affiliations.** Instead of "I'm a progressive," try "I care about reducing inequality." Instead of "I'm a conservative," try "I value personal responsibility and limited government." Values can guide your thinking without requiring you to adopt a package deal of pre-approved positions.

**Practice intellectual honesty.** When someone from your usual group proposes something, ask yourself if you'd support it if the other side proposed it. If not, why not? Be willing to say out loud which parts of your group's platform you disagree with.

**Form temporary coalitions.** You can work with someone on climate policy without signing up for their entire political agenda. Single-issue cooperation doesn't require tribal membership.

**Reward nuance.** When someone in your group questions conventional wisdom, treat it as a sign of independent thought, not betrayal. Disagreement is how we avoid groupthink.

**Be a person who holds ideas, not a member who holds identities.** There's a difference between "I'm a climate activist" and "I'm a person who thinks climate change is a serious problem." The first makes the label central to who you are. The second keeps the idea as something you think, not something you are.

## The Cost of Getting This Wrong

Group identity isn't going away. We're social creatures, and belonging matters to us. But we can be more thoughtful about how we let it shape our thinking.

The alternative is a world where we defend positions we don't believe, attack people who mostly agree with us, and make everyone worse off in pursuit of tribal victory. We can see that world around us right now. It's exhausting, and it's getting us nowhere.

Maybe it's time to hold our beliefs a little more loosely and our identities a little less tightly. Maybe it's time to think for ourselves again.
